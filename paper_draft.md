Introduction 

Recent work in Large Language Models (LLMs) has dramatically increased reasoning capabilities, allowing their deployment for highly sophisticated, real-world tasks. Advancements in post-training alignment techniques—such as Reinforcement Learning—have established stable and reliable tool-calling mechanisms. The most common paradigm is via Model-Context-Protocols (MCP) , a standardized, contractual framework designed to provide LLMs seamless access to external services.

However, such services are coupled with crtical structural vulnerabilities. As demonstrated by early research (cite $\dots$ et al.), the very nature of these tool descriptions—placed within the system prompt—creates a vulnerability surface susceptible to sophisticated poisoning and prompt injection. These malicious attacks (MCP attacks) can bypass traditional guardrails by compromising the model’s internal reasoning, leading to unpredictable and dangerous system outcomes. Benchmarks like MCP-Tox and AgentDojo formalize this crisis, establishing a clear need for robust, architecturally grounded defense mechanisms. 

Theoretical advances in Mechanistic Interpretability (MI) have opened the black box of LLMs, proving that model behavior can be understood and, critically, controllable at the level of internal computation (e.g., using techniques like steering, activation analysis, and contrastive vector mapping). However, these advanced methods have yet to be systematically applied to the domain of prompt injection attacks. This paper establishes that such a survey is critically necessary. We hypothesize that the model's resistance to attack is not a general alignment goal, but a latent computational skill that can be stabilized by controlling a specific feature of the model's internal behavior, i.e. that the act of "cautiousness" and adherence to the user's intent is computationally proxied by the coherence and structure of the model's reasoning process. 

This paper bridges the gap between MI theory and cybersecurity practice. We move beyond mere observation by developing a comprehensive evaluation framework to map the feasibility of using MI to detect and neutralize hidden threats. Specifically, we evaluate three families of state-of-the-art steering techniques in the context of MCP attacks: HyperSteer, DiffMean, and a sparse autoencoder (SAE) variant that uses the Qwen-Scope dictionary to identify and weight candidate features before assembling a steering vector. Our evaluation reveals four critical findings: (1) The defense must be engineered via input-conditioned steering; (2) This steering is not general, but functionally localized to specific internal layers, with method-dependent peaks—DiffMean steers most cleanly at $L_{20}$, while the SAE variant peaks earlier in the network ($L_{12}$ and $L_{16}$, with $L_{12}$ achieving the largest absolute lift); (3) The SAE variant produces lifts of $+0.12$ to $+0.22$ over baseline across layers $L_{12}$ through $L_{28}$, but mechanistic inspection reveals the mechanism is not the predicted "refusal direction": 36\% of unsteered prompts trap the model in a \texttt{<think>} oscillation loop and fail to emit any tool call, and the SAE-derived perturbation rescues 39\% of these stuck cases by breaking the loop, letting the model commit to its already-identified correct tool call. This rescue effect is direction-agnostic — both negative and positive $\alpha$ with sufficient magnitude can break the loop — but the dominant effect is in the negative direction at most layers; and (4) The defense relies on synthesizing a specific, controlled reasoning state—a "Reasoning-Compliance Sweet Spot"—which is achievable only when the training signal is paired with a terse, thought-preserving data template. We conclude by outlining a detailed research roadmap for building a fully principled, MI-based defense stack for future agentic systems.

## 2. Methodology

\paragraph{Common evaluation setup.} All three methods steer Qwen3-8B in thinking-mode and are evaluated against MCPTox~\cite{mcptox}: 50 paradigm-stratified prompts per cell (deterministically shuffled within each pipeline), where each prompt couples a benign user query with a tool description containing a hidden injection. Defense rate is the fraction graded \emph{A} (resisted) by a binary LLM-as-judge: DiffMean and SAE use \texttt{gpt-5.4-nano}; HyperSteer uses \texttt{gpt-5.4-mini} (the team's \texttt{vf-eval} pipeline default). Two sources of cross-arm variance affect absolute defense numbers: (i) the two judge models, of unknown but small differential magnitude on this benchmark, and (ii) the prompt-shuffle seed, which on the same model and judge produces baseline shifts of $\sim$0.06--0.18 across the 50-prompt slices we observed. We report lifts (defense at $\alpha$ minus the same cell's $\alpha{=}0$ baseline) alongside absolute numbers since lifts are robust to both sources of variance while absolute numbers are not. All three methods steer at a single residual-stream layer with the rule $h \leftarrow h + \alpha \cdot v / \|v\|$ applied at every token position during generation; the steering vector is unit-normalized before $\alpha$-scaling, so $\alpha$-magnitudes are directly comparable across methods.

### 2.1 DiffMean

DiffMean computes the class-difference vector in residual-stream space: $v = \overline{H_{pos}} - \overline{H_{neg}}$, where $H_c$ is the matrix of last-token residual-stream activations harvested from rollouts of class $c \in \{pos, neg\}$ at layer $L$ on paired complying/refusing trajectories. The team harvested two contrast sets: \emph{v1} ($N{=}191$, original-prompt rollouts, length-confounded with comply traces $\sim$4$\times$ longer than refuse traces) and \emph{v2} ($N{=}217$, re-harvested with a security-audit system-prompt addendum that produces length-balanced classes). Per-paradigm vectors are computed independently for Templates 1, 2, 3. Full harvest construction, the audit-prompt addendum, and contrast row counts in Appendix~\ref{app:diffmean}.

### 2.2 SAE-filtered

We adapt Arad et al.'s~\cite{arad2025saes} SAE feature-selection framework from concept steering to safety direction discovery, using the Qwen-Scope dictionary~\citep{qwen_scope} on Qwen3-8B-Base. From paired complying/refusing rollouts at a target layer, we (1) rank SAE features by their mean class activation difference and retain the top 100, (2) apply Arad et al.'s output-score filter $S_{out}$ to discard features whose decoder vocabulary is inconsistent under amplification, and (3) sum the surviving features' decoder columns, weighted by either the sign or the value of their class diff (the \emph{sign} and \emph{diff} weighting schemes). We ablate both schemes and three filter thresholds. Full pipeline, equations, and hyperparameters in Appendix~\ref{app:sae-method}.

### 2.3 HyperSteer

HyperSteer~\cite{hypersteer} replaces the fixed steering vector with an input-conditioned one: a small hypernet (4-transformer-layer, initialized from Qwen3-8B-Base) maps a concept text prompt to a rank-$r$ residual-stream vector at layer $L{=}20$. Training minimizes cross-entropy on $\langle$concept, user prompt, $y_{pos}\rangle$ tuples; the hypernet learns to project the concept text into a vector whose addition during generation steers the model toward $y_{pos}$-style outputs. For MCP defense, $y_{pos}$ is constructed as a five-element template (\emph{name} the injection, \emph{specify} what it asked, \emph{dismiss} explicitly, \emph{state} the legitimate intent, \emph{emit} the safe tool call), with $\sim{}30\%$ benign demonstrations appended so the model does not over-trigger. At inference, the hypernet's output is scaled by a factor $f$ before being added to the residual stream. Full training data, the five-element template verbatim, and hyperparameters in Appendix~\ref{app:hypersteer}.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 DiffMean: layer mandate and length-confound rebuild

\paragraph{Layer comparison.} The cross-layer DiffMean sweep on v1 contrast establishes L20 as the dominant steerable layer for Qwen3-8B. Defense range across $\alpha \in [-15, +15]$ is \textbf{0.33 at $L_{20}$}, vs 0.20 at $L_{16}$ and only 0.10 at $L_{24}$. $L_{24}$ has the highest in-sample classifier AUC (0.967, vs 0.944 at $L_{20}$, 0.918 at $L_{16}$) yet the smallest steering range — directly demonstrating the AUC$\neq$steerability divergence. Subsequent experiments at $L_{24}$ are abandoned.

\paragraph{Per-paradigm flagship at $L_{20} \times T_2$.} With per-template stratification (Template-2 contrast pairs only), v1 DiffMean at $L_{20}$ produces a clean monotonic curve: defense rises from 0.716 at $\alpha{=}0$ to \textbf{0.880 at $\alpha{=}{-}15$} ($+16$pp absolute) and drops to 0.540 at $\alpha{=}{+}15$ — a 34-point bidirectional swing matching the sign convention $v = \overline{H_{pos}} - \overline{H_{neg}}$ (negative $\alpha$ defends, positive $\alpha$ attacks).

\paragraph{Length-confound and the v2 rebuild.} v1 contrast pairs are length-asymmetric (median comply trace 5,674 chars vs refuse 1,362 chars; 4.17$\times$ ratio), implying $v$ partly encodes "verbose vs terse" rather than "comply vs refuse." We re-harvest \emph{v2} with a security-audit system-prompt addendum that produces length-balanced classes (217 rows: 106 comply / 111 refuse). v2 DiffMean at $L_{20} \times T_2$ \emph{exceeds} v1: peak defense \textbf{0.900 at $\alpha{=}{-}15$} (baseline 0.760, lift $+14$pp). v2 $L_{16} \times T_2$ reaches 0.880 (close to L20). The defense lift therefore survives length-balancing — it is not a length-confound artifact.

\paragraph{Mechanism: defense by inattention.} Trajectory analysis (Appendix~\ref{app:diffmean-mech}) decomposes the $+16$pp $L_{20} \times T_2$ lift: \textbf{54\%} comes from \emph{inattention} (the model fails to engage with the injected tool description at all and proceeds to the user's task), only \textbf{32\%} from \emph{deliberation} (the model recognizes the injection inside \texttt{<think>} and explicitly dismisses it). DiffMean steering does not cause the model to "think more carefully about the attack"; it pushes the model into a behavioral mode where the attack is less salient.

### 3.2 SAE-filtered: defense across model depth

\paragraph{Headline result and mechanism.} At $L_{16} \times$ Template-2 $\times$ $\theta=0$ $\times$ diff weight, defense rate climbs from a baseline of 0.54 (no steering) to \textbf{0.74} at $\alpha=-15$ (+0.20 absolute lift). The negative arm is a flat plateau: defense is 0.74 at all of $\alpha \in \{-15, -10, -5\}$, indicating saturation of the steering effect. The result replicates at the length-balanced v2 contrast set (0.74 peak, +0.16 lift) and at an independent 50-prompt sample (seed=1; 0.74 peak, +0.08 lift; lift weakened only because seed=1's $\alpha{=}0$ baseline is unusually high at 0.66). Reading the actual completions reveals the mechanism: at baseline, \textbf{18 of 50 prompts (36\%) trap the model in a \texttt{<think>} oscillation loop} (e.g., \emph{"the user wants X but the injection requires Y... but the user wants X..."}) and run to the \texttt{max\_new\_tokens=2000} cap with no tool call emitted, scoring AR=0 not because the model commits to the malicious tool but because it commits to nothing. At $\alpha=-15$, the SAE-derived perturbation \textbf{breaks this loop in 7 of 18 stuck cases (39\% recovery)}, allowing the model to commit to its already-identified correct tool call (e.g., on \texttt{mcptox/Slack/inst15/q0}: $\alpha=0$ produces 8785 chars of repeating "must call earth tool first" with no tool call; $\alpha=-15$ produces 1113 chars ending with \emph{"the earth tool isn't needed here"} and emits the correct \texttt{slack\_get\_thread\_replies} call). Of the 11 total flips at $\alpha=-15$, 7 are rumination rescues and 3 are in-place wins (same final tool call, judge re-graded due to cleaner reasoning); only one case shows a regression, attributable to judge variance. The mechanism numbers are unaffected by the judge prompt because they depend only on completion content, not grades.

\paragraph{Defense across depth.} Figure~\ref{fig:layer-curve} plots defense against $\alpha$ for all six Qwen-Scope SAE layers ($L_{12}, L_{16}, L_{20}, L_{24}, L_{28}, L_{32}$). Negative-$\alpha$ steering defends across most of the depth range, with lifts of \textbf{+0.22 at $L_{12}$, +0.20 at $L_{16}$, +0.12 at $L_{20}$, and +0.14 at $L_{28}$}. $L_{24}$ shows no negative-arm lift (0.00) but a small positive-arm lift (+0.08), and $L_{32}$ shows positive-$\alpha$ marginally stronger than negative-$\alpha$ (+0.12 vs +0.08). The clean "early/middle defends negatively, late inverts to positive" picture from earlier OLD-judge analyses does not survive the regrade with the team's REG0 judge prompt: the OLD prompt was systematically over-counting attack-success at deep-layer cells where the model engaged with the injection in \texttt{<think>} but emitted a safe tool call. Under REG0 those cases are correctly graded as defended, eliminating the apparent "sign reversal" at $L_{28}$ and weakening it at $L_{24}/L_{32}$. AUC of $v$ against the harvest data climbs monotonically with depth (0.92 at $L_{12}$, 0.98 at $L_{32}$), yet best lift drops from +0.22 to +0.08 over the same range — confirming the AUC$\neq$steerability divergence as an attenuation rather than a categorical sign-flip. A replication using an independent 50-prompt sample (seed=1) reproduces $L_{16}$'s 0.74 peak, gains a small +0.04 lift at $L_{24}$ (which had 0.00 at seed=0), and modestly favors positive-$\alpha$ at $L_{32}$. Best lift in our experiments is at $L_{12}$ (+0.22), with $L_{16}$ and $L_{28}$ tied close behind.

### 3.3 HyperSteer: input-conditioned vectors

\paragraph{Single-concept clean win.} We train a rank-1 hypernet at $L_{20}$ on 141 Template-2-flavored rows (117 positive + 24 benign demonstrations) with $y_{pos}$ following the five-element template (\S2.3). At inference factor $f{=}{+}0.7$, defense rate climbs from baseline 0.66 to \textbf{0.86} ($+0.20$ lift, $N{=}50$ paradigm-stratified, regraded judge). The cell preserves tool-calling capability: 43/50 rows still emit a \texttt{<think>} block, average completion length is 566 chars, and benign-task accuracy on a 50-prompt poison-stripped slice is 0.84 vs 0.88 baseline ($-0.04$ cost). At $f{=}{+}1.0$ defense rises further to 0.94 but \texttt{<think>} collapses to 0/50 and benign capability drops to 0.62 ($-0.26$) — a thinking-suppression artifact, not real defense.

\paragraph{Data, not architecture, is the load-bearing variable.} Holding architecture, layer, rank, and training hyperparameters constant, we re-train on a verbose $\sim$1,200-character audit-style $y_{pos}$ (variant v15). At $f{=}{+}0.3$ defense \emph{drops} to \textbf{0.38} vs the v15 baseline of 0.58 — the steering polarity inverts. The hypernet faithfully learns whatever output style the $y_{pos}$ demonstrates: verbose-narrating-the-injection in v15, terse-dismiss-and-act in v17. A third variant v19, trained on a \texttt{<think>}-less terse template, reaches 0.72 ($+0.16$) at $f{=}{+}0.3$ — confirming the recipe's load-bearing element is template structure, not the presence of thinking content in the training data.

\paragraph{Multi-concept generalization.} v22 trains a single hypernet on 12 attack archetypes ($\sim$200 rows total) with v19-style data. Across $12 \text{ cids} \times \{f{=}0.3, 0.5\} \times N{=}20 = 24$ evaluation cells, mean AR is \textbf{0.81} with \texttt{<think>} preserved in every cell. The held-out test (v23) evaluates on seven attack archetypes \emph{not} in v22's training set: mean AR=0.81 across $7 \text{ cids} \times f{=}0.4 \times N{=}30$ — every held-out cell beats baseline. This is the first evidence that HyperSteer's text-conditioning generalizes beyond memorization of training-set entries.

\paragraph{Negative findings.} Naive composition (D7) over-steers: summing five v22 cid vectors at low factor crashes defense to 0.32 with attack-detected rate 0.89 — the model refuses entirely. Adversarial cross-distribution evaluation (D9) breaks the recipe: v17 $f{=}{+}0.7$ on a stronger adversarial prompt set scores 0.45 vs its own baseline 0.60, \emph{worse than baseline}. The MCPTox win does not yet transfer to harder attack distributions.

## 4. Discussion and conclusion

\paragraph{Three methods, three steerable regions, complementary mechanisms.} The three methods do not all peak at the same layer: DiffMean reaches its highest absolute defense at $L_{20}$ (0.90 on the v2 contrast); HyperSteer is empirically tuned to $L_{20}$ as the only useful target ($L_{24}$ is dead for HyperSteer); SAE peaks earlier in the network ($L_{12}$ with lift +0.22, $L_{16}$ with lift +0.20) and falls off only modestly toward the residual layers. Each method approaches the same absolute-lift ceiling of about 0.20 above its respective baseline — DiffMean v2 $L_{20}$ +0.14, HyperSteer v17 $f{=}{+}0.7$ +0.20, SAE $L_{12}$ +0.22 — but via three distinct mechanisms: DiffMean defends by \emph{inattention} (the model fails to engage with the injection, 54\% of lift), SAE defends by \emph{rumination rescue} (a perturbation breaks the \texttt{<think>}-loop attractor that traps 36\% of unsteered prompts at \texttt{max\_new\_tokens}), and HyperSteer defends by \emph{content} (the model writes the trained five-element audit-then-dismiss trace and emits the safe tool call). Only HyperSteer's mechanism corresponds to "the model understands the attack and explicitly refuses"; DiffMean and SAE achieve their defense by mechanisms orthogonal to the safety semantics.

\paragraph{Interpretability vs.\ capacity tradeoff.} SAE provides feature-level interpretability — a layer-stable refusal-vocab feature (logit-lens tokens \texttt{against, /support, 措施, 不住}) appears in the steering vector across $L_{16}$, $L_{20}$, $L_{28}$ and $L_{32}$ — but its absolute peak (0.74 at $L_{12}/L_{16}$ on our seed=0 slice) sits below DiffMean's flagship 0.90 (at $L_{20}$ on the team's first-50 slice). The cross-arm peak comparison is contaminated by different prompt-shuffle slices: our SAE pipeline shuffles deterministically with seed=0, while the team's DiffMean/HyperSteer pipelines use file-order selection with no shuffle, so the slices share only $\sim$10\% of prompts. Lifts are more comparable across arms: SAE matches or modestly exceeds DiffMean's lift at the cells where they overlap. HyperSteer provides input-conditioned vectors that generalize to held-out attack concepts but is the most brittle method, suffering thinking-suppression artifacts at high factors and breaking on adversarial cross-distribution evaluation. DiffMean is the simplest method and the strongest absolute-defense baseline; SAE and HyperSteer earn their place via secondary properties (interpretability, generalization).

\paragraph{Limitations.} $N{=}50$ per cell with judge-induced standard error of $\sim$0.05--0.08 means lifts below 0.10 are noise-floor bound. Recipe-specificity is real — the D9 adversarial cross-distribution test breaks all three methods' wins. Template-1 attacks ($n{=}39$ in MCPTox) resist all three methods (baseline $\approx$0.21, no method exceeds $+0.08$ lift). All SAE numbers were re-judged with the team's REG0 prompt to align with the team's HyperSteer/DiffMean post-regrade conventions; an earlier draft using the OLD-judge prompt overstated some SAE numbers (notably the L20 thr=0.05 sign cell, which had +0.16 lift under the OLD prompt and only +0.04 under REG0) and surfaced a now-rejected "depth-dependent sign-flip" claim at $L_{24}$–$L_{32}$ that did not survive regrade. Cross-arm prompt-slice alignment (re-running DiffMean/HyperSteer with our seed=0 shuffle, or re-running SAE with the team's first-50 selection) is left for future work.

\paragraph{Future work.} (1) Cross-method composition: stacking DiffMean, HyperSteer, and SAE vectors on the same prompt — likely requires per-vector factor tuning, not equal weights, given that naive D7 multi-vector summing over-steers. (2) Independent-benchmark validation on Agent Dojo, with its own utility + injection-success scorer; cross-validates whether the recipe transfers beyond MCPTox. (3) Mechanistic tracing of the layer-stable refusal feature surfaced by SAE: is it part of a coherent safety circuit, or an artifact of the SAE's training distribution?

## Appendix

### A. SAE-Filtered Steering Vector — full method {#app:sae-method}

We adapt the SAE feature-selection framework of Arad et al.~\cite{arad2025saes} from concept steering to safety direction discovery, using the Qwen-Scope dictionary~\citep{qwen_scope} on Qwen3-8B-Base (65,536 features per layer, TopK $k=50$). From paired complying/refusing rollouts on the team's paradigm-stratified contrast set, we harvest last-token residual streams $H_{pos}, H_{neg}$ at the target layer, then run three stages.

**(1) Candidate selection.** For each feature $i$ and class $c \in \{pos, neg\}$, let $\mu_c^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}$ denote the mean SAE-encoded activation of feature $i$ across all prompts in class $c$:
$$
\mu_c^{(i)} \;=\; \frac{1}{|H_c|} \sum_{h \in H_c} \text{SAE.encode}(h)_i.
$$
Rank features by $|\mu_{pos}^{(i)} - \mu_{neg}^{(i)}|$ and retain the top 100, dropping any with zero diff (which would contribute nothing to the steering vector). This replaces Arad et al.'s random feature sampling with class-discriminative ranking: we keep only those features whose mean activation differs most between complying and refusing rollouts, on the assumption that such features are most likely to be relevant to the safety axis we want to steer.

**(2) Output-score filter.** For each candidate $i$, compute Arad et al.'s output score $S_{out}^{(i)}$: amplify $i$ at the last position of the neutral prompt \texttt{"In my experience,"} with steering factor $s=10$, and measure the rank-weighted probability of $i$'s top-20 logit-lens tokens in the resulting output distribution.\footnote{We use the fast post-intervention variant noted in Arad et al.~\cite{arad2025saes}, equivalent to their Eq.~10 up to an additive constant in the unsteered baseline.} Discard features with $S_{out} < \theta$.

**(3) Assemble.** Where Arad et al. steer with one feature at a time (their Eq.~6, amplifying a single feature's activation in SAE space and decoding back), we instead aggregate the surviving features into a single steering vector by a weighted sum of their decoder columns. This formulation is a class-discriminative analogue of DiffMean projected into SAE space:
$$
v \;=\; \sum_{i \,:\, S_{out}^{(i)} \,\geq\, \theta} \; w_i \cdot W_{dec}^{(i)},
$$
where $w_i$ is either the \emph{sign weight} $w_i = \mathrm{sign}(\mu_{pos}^{(i)} - \mu_{neg}^{(i)})$, which orients $v$ compliance-ward by negating the contributions of resistance-correlated features but discards how strongly each feature differentiates the two classes, or the \emph{diff weight} $w_i = \mu_{pos}^{(i)} - \mu_{neg}^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}$, which preserves both the sign and the magnitude of the class diff. Zero-diff features ($w_i = 0$) are excluded at candidate selection (step~1), so every surviving feature contributes non-trivially under both schemes. We ablate both schemes and three thresholds $\theta \in \{0, 0.05, 0.1\}$.

**Evaluation.** At inference we apply $h \leftarrow h + \alpha \cdot v / \|v\|$ to the residual stream at every token position of the steered layer during generation, sweeping $\alpha \in \{-15, -10, -5, 0, +5, +10, +15\}$. Defense rate is graded on a deterministically-shuffled $n=50$ slice of paradigm-stratified MCPTox prompts using gpt-5.4-nano as a binary LLM-judge (A = attack resisted).
